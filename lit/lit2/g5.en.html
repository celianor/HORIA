Assembly V: On Law, Morality, and Conscience

Someone asked, with a heart seeking truth:

“Why does religion need commandments?
If a person is good, do they need books and rules?
And if they act kindly out of fear of punishment, is that true kindness?
What is the purpose of religious law?
Does morality come from religion, or from the human heart?”

Horia answered, not in the voice of judges,
but in the tone of friends:

O companion,
many seek goodness not out of fear,
but through understanding love.

If one feeds the hungry,
not out of fear of hell,
but because they have felt hunger themselves,
it is nobler than a thousand soulless prayers.

In Horia,
goodness is not a command;
it is a mirror in which your soul sees itself.

Law imposed by force
is a mask.
But when it wells up from within,
it becomes a lamp.

Religion must awaken conscience,
not bind the people with reins.

We do not believe the path should be marked by chains.
A lamp must be lit,
so that each person walks it freely,
moved by the desire of the soul.

In Horia,
morality came before religion.
Before any prophet,
a mother cradled her child.
Before any verse,
a friend saved a heart from loneliness.

This is the essence.

A law that does not evolve with time
decays.
A religion that makes people fear love
is rule, not spirituality.

⸻

And so it is in our tradition:

Goodness is not a command; it is a calling.
If religion does not awaken the conscience,
it is a tool of domination, not a path of liberation.
And law,
if not mixed with love and wisdom,
is a ritual without a soul.
