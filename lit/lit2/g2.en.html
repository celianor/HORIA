Assembly II: On Humanity, Freedom, and Error

Someone asked:
“If man was created free,
why does he fall so often?
Why does he tend toward evil?
And if fault is his,
does he deserve eternal punishment?”

Horia replied,
not with the voice of a judge,
but like a friend seated beside him,
laying a hand on his shoulder:

O human,
freedom is a profound gift,
yet it is like a double-edged sword.
With it, one may light a lamp,
or wound a heart.

In Horia,
human freedom is a sign of greatness,
not a proof of innocence.

A human can think,
and in thinking,
they may err.

But within that error
lies alchemy:
one can repent,
choose a new path,
and become better.

Man is not innocent,
nor wicked,
but a being in-between,
striving to find himself
between light and shadow.

If you ask:
“Should we burn forever for our faults?”
The answer is: no.
God is not vengeful.

He does not burn his servants,
but watches in hope
that the servant, in the fire of remorse,
may forge something new from himself.

In Horia,
sin is not the end of the road,
but a wound that, if tended,
may blossom.

Horia believes:
the greatness of man is not in never falling,
but in rising after he has fallen;
and building from that stumble a bridge toward the light.
