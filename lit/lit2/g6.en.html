Assembly VI: On Death, Liberation, and the Soul’s Destiny

One of the attendants,
with a noble sorrow in his eyes,
gently asked:

“Is death an end or a passage?
Is there a soul that endures,
or does all fall silent
when the body returns to the earth?
And if there is a soul,
what is its fate?
What is liberation?
And is it something to hope for?”

Horia spoke,
not offering easy promises,
but out of love for humanity’s honest unknowing:

Companion,
in the depths of the heart,
a light of hope shines,
for such love,
such thought and devotion,
do not vanish into the soil.

In our path,
death is not a wall;
it is a gate.
Not an end,
but the opening of another chapter of being.

You are a drop
that rose from the ocean,
and you will return to it.
Not to be lost,
but to embrace the whole sea within you.

Your body is made of dust
born from a dying star,
and in your soul
echo songs
that are not only of this earth.

In our eyes,
death is not the punishment of life,
but a passing through an old skin
into the unseen mysteries of the soul.

If you ask:
“Is there a heaven and a hell?”
We say:
heaven and hell begin here,
and from within us.
Hell is a dark heart
kindled by hatred.
And heaven
is a lamp lit by love.

Those who,
in their lives,
brought light to others’ souls,
have taken a step toward liberation
even before death.

In Horia,
liberation is not the reward of worship,
but the fruit of awakening.
Not a bargain with the sky,
but a blooming from within.

If the soul blends with love,
it will escape the body’s cage like a fragrance,
and return to the source
where the first song of being rose.

And in the way of the good:
Death is not silence;
it is liberation.
And liberation belongs to those
who have lived love,
not merely praised its name.
