Continuing the Comparative Analysis of Horia with Islam, Christianity, and Buddhism Based on Kantian Moral Philosophy

4. Freedom, Autonomy, and Moral Agency

Kant considers freedom a prerequisite for morality: only a free will can be moral.

  • In Horia, freedom is a foundational element, and no law is sacred unless it is accepted by reason and the collective mind.
  • In Islam, freedom is defined within servitude; Sharia determines its boundaries.
  • In Christianity, while individual liberty is respected, salvation depends on faith and divine grace.
  • Buddhism defines freedom more inwardly, as detachment and release from desire.

Conclusion: Only Horia, like Kant, considers freedom the primary condition for morality and legislation.

5. The Relationship Between Religion, Politics, and Society

Kant advocates for republicanism, public law, and a moral legal society.

  • Horia builds religious and social structures on criticism, human dignity, and collective reason.
  • In Islam, religion and state are often unified, with Sharia forming the foundation of political order.
  • In Christianity, the relationship between religion and politics varies among denominations.
  • Buddhism has rarely engaged with political structures and remains largely introspective.

Conclusion: In terms of social structure, Horia aligns more closely with Kant’s vision of moral republicanism than other religions.

6. Death and the Afterlife

Kant believes in an afterlife as a necessary condition for the full realization of justice.

  • Horia also considers the afterlife a physical and rational continuation of existence—not a field of divine reward and punishment.
  • Islam and Christianity view the afterlife as a realm of recompense and judgment.
  • Buddhism believes in reincarnation or final extinction (Nirvana), not an individual afterlife.

Conclusion: Horia’s view of death is more aligned with Kantian moral idealism than with traditional punitive models.

General Conclusion

In this comparative analysis grounded in Kantian philosophy, Horia can be regarded as “the closest religion to practical reason.”

  • Self-grounded ethics,
  • Free and rational faith,
  • Republican structure,
  • And a meaningful view of death,

These are the features that position Horia within the global religious landscape as a model of the “religion of reason”—a vision Kant described as a seemingly unattainable but ultimately realizable ideal.
