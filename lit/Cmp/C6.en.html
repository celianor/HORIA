A Religion for Everyone: Cosmopolitanism and Coexistence in Horia Against Traditional Religious Boundaries – Part Two

Continuation of Part One

4. Emerging Spiritualities: Global but Shallow and Rootless

New spiritual movements such as New Age, by promoting love, peace, and cosmopolitanism, try to go beyond traditional constraints. However:

  • They often lack philosophical structure and intellectual coherence.
  • They promote more a consumerist cultural aesthetic than a deep cosmopolitanism.
  • They emphasize individual experience without connection to justice, social structure, or sustainable coexistence.

As a result, although they do not build boundaries, they also do not provide a foundation for meaningful coexistence.

5. Horia: A Religion with a Global Identity, Multiple Languages, and Authentic Humanism

The Horia religion presents itself as the “celebration of life for everyone” — and this is not just a slogan:

  • No language, ethnicity, or nation is sacred. Every language is a language of truth and every human being, regardless of origin, deserves dignity.
  • It does not merely tolerate cultural diversity but considers it the essence of human richness: “each culture is a unique instrument in the symphony of life.”
  • No ethnicity, nation, or race is privileged; being human and living consciously are the only criteria.
  • Coexistence is emphasized not only with other humans but with the entire ecosystem and existence.
  • The concepts of “celebration of life,” “world harmonies,” and “house without doors” in Horia’s texts clearly express the rejection of boundaries and divisions.

In Horia, differences are not threats but the instruments of the symphony. What matters is “being,” not “where you come from.” Boundaries are not erased to create artificial uniformity; rather, diversity is recognized with love, and the religion plays the role of host to the common celebration.

6. Peace in Horia: An Open Embrace, Not Just the Absence of Violence

Horia offers not only a timeless and universal teaching but also emphasizes empathy among humans of all colors, languages, and lived experiences. Faith in Horia does not wear ethnic costumes; race, nationality, or mother tongue do not confer spiritual advantage.

Unlike many traditions that defensively confront the “other” and understand peace as the absence of violence, Horia:

  • Speaks not of forced tolerance but of conscious harmony.
  • Infuses an aesthetic dimension into peace; peace is not merely a moral command but an artistic and poetic experience. Horia integrates tools for intercultural dialogue (poetry, music, language, symbols) into its structure.
  • Cosmopolitanism is not a political or moral message but an epistemological structure of the religion: no truth is national, no spirituality is local.
  • Peace means active coexistence with diversity, not just tolerance of it.
  • The concept of “alienation” becomes meaningless because the alienating boundaries collapse in Horia’s thought.
  • The call to cosmopolitanism is central to the narrative, with a poetic rather than preachy approach: a world without borders but not without roots. Boundaries are not only external but also rooted in minds — and Horia invites crossing these.

Conclusion: A Religion for the World, Not for a Particular Tribe

A religion that wants to speak to contemporary humans and be meaningful today must recognize boundaries — not to strengthen them, but to consciously cross them. Then it must transcend them — not by conquest, but by celebration. Religions still trapped in notions of “chosen people,” “sacred language,” “unsaved other,” or “promised nation” cannot be foundations for global peace.

Horia is not a religion for a tribe but a spirituality for all humanity; it seeks peace in difference and cosmopolitanism in harmony.

Horia promises a religion that says:

“Wherever there is a human, there is a place for celebration; and every language that speaks with kindness is a Horia language.”
