Painting on the Water

It was sunset.
The sky, like a child’s sketchbook, was full of faded, hidden colors:
a bit of misty white and grey,
a tired shade of gold,
and blue lines drifting from the day’s dreams.

The yard was quiet. Only the distant chirping of a sparrow and the soft rustle of a tree calming itself in the breeze.

Diane, as always, stood peacefully by the pool, a gentle smile on her face.
In her fingers, she held a red petal from the old geranium in the garden,
the way poets hold a rare word before they speak it aloud.

Then, without saying a word, she placed the petal on the water.
The surface of the pool trembled;
like the moment you suddenly fall in love with someone.

“Look, Dara… it's like a painting… on the water.”

Dara, younger and more cheerful, ran closer.
His wide, questioning eyes sparkled.

“It’s so pretty… But look! The shape’s changing… The ripples are carrying the painting away.”

A gentle breeze passed through the jasmine bushes.
The water trembled like a longing heart.
The image of the petal tilted, stretched, faded…

Diane frowned slightly—not out of anger, but a soft sorrow for something she couldn’t keep.

“But I wanted it to stay… just like that.”

Dara shrugged, as if he had lived many more years.

“Everything changes, Diane. Like this picture… But that doesn’t mean it isn’t beautiful, right?”

I, watching them from the other side of the yard, felt that time paused for a moment.
Like when someone speaks a truth without realizing it.

Diane sat down, hugging her knees.
Her voice turned soft and slightly sad, like the glow of a lamp in the night:

“So… things that go away can still be beautiful?”

Dara said:

“Yes… If you try to keep them, they disappear. But if you just watch them, and let them go… they stay in your heart.”

Another breeze arrived.
As if from those worlds Diane sometimes dreamed of.
The petal spun, drifted away, vanished.

But Diane’s smile stayed on her face, in that twilight.

And then she said, as if speaking to the wind:

“Then let the world paint… even on the water.”

And I still believe that the most beautiful paintings in the world
are drawn upon water…
and remain in the heart.

Some images are not meant to stay…
They come to stir the soul,
like a painting on the water,
beautiful while they last,
and when they leave,
they leave a trace of light within you.

Don’t cling to what must go;
let it go,
so that something deeper remains in you:
acceptance,
kindness,
and the memory of that radiant moment,
when your heart smiled… for no reason.
